## The story

### Section 1: You Don't Actually Want Everything

Lately, I've been thinking a lot about this journey we've been on together. What started as a small idea — sharing English, thoughts, and quiet reflections — has slowly grown into something that feels truly meaningful. Every message, every comment, every moment you chose to listen has become part of this path.

And now we're getting closer to 100,000 subscribers, which still feels a little unreal to say out loud. I feel grateful, excited… and also reflective. Because growth doesn't always feel loud or confident. Sometimes it feels quiet, slow, and surprisingly fragile.

When you work on something for a long time, often behind the scenes, you don't always notice the progress right away. And yet, even when things are moving forward, there are moments where doubt quietly appears — not because something is wrong, but simply because you care.

Have you ever been working toward something quietly… and then suddenly felt unsure — even though nothing actually went wrong?

Maybe it happened in a very normal moment. You're sitting at a café. You bring your notebook. You write down a few goals. They make sense to you. They feel realistic. Calm. Then you look around. At the next table, someone is talking confidently in English with a foreign client. On the other side, a group is discussing their startup idea. Someone else looks busy, focused, important. You look back at your notebook. And your goal suddenly feels… small.

I've noticed something in myself in moments like that. I don't suddenly want to quit. I don't suddenly hate my path. I just start thinking: "Maybe I should aim higher." "Maybe this is too simple." "Maybe I'm not thinking big enough."

Sometimes we don't leave our path because it's wrong. We leave because it doesn't look impressive enough.

We say we want growth. We say we want improvement. But often, what we really want is reassurance that we are moving fast enough. And speed is very visible in other people. But progress on your own path? That's private. Quiet. Invisible.

I once met a young woman who told me she had been learning English for almost a year. Slowly. Consistently. She wasn't fluent. She wasn't advanced. But she showed up every week. One day she attended a networking event. She heard someone speak perfectly. That night she told herself, "I'm so behind. Maybe I should try something else." Nothing changed about her effort. Only her comparison did. And that comparison almost erased one year of quiet discipline.

Staying on your path requires something simple but difficult: You have to decide what matters before the world tells you what should matter.

Most people don't fail by choosing wrong. They fail by choosing too many times. And sometimes, the bravest thing you can do… is stay.

### Section 2: The Small Decisions That Move You Sideways

You rarely wake up and say, "I'm going to leave my path today." It doesn't happen like that. It happens in quiet, reasonable decisions.

You plan to practice English after work. Nothing big. Just twenty minutes. You even feel slightly proud that you're being consistent. But the day feels long. Your body feels heavy. You tell yourself, "I'll just sit down for five minutes first." You make tea. You look at your to-do list. You rearrange something small on your desk. You're not avoiding. You're preparing.

Then you think, "Maybe I should finish that small task first so my mind feels clear." That small task becomes two. Then three. By the time you open your notebook, your energy is lower. Your focus is thinner. And the voice appears: "I'll do it properly tomorrow." Nothing dramatic happened. But you moved sideways.

I've noticed something uncomfortable in my own life. The days I drift away from what matters to me are not chaotic days. They are organized days filled with small, practical actions. That's what makes it tricky. Because everything you did was reasonable. You answered emails. You helped someone. You cleaned something. You stayed busy. But you didn't move forward.

Sideways movement feels productive. That's why it's hard to detect.

We think abandoning our path will look like laziness. But often it looks like responsibility. You're doing things. You're just not doing the right things.

There was a period when I wanted to build a steady reading habit again. Just ten pages a day. The first week went well. The second week, I missed one evening. It felt small. The third week, I missed two days because I was "catching up" on other tasks. By the fourth week, I still told myself I cared — but I hadn't opened the book in days. I didn't quit. I postponed. And postponing is quiet. No announcement. No decision. Just delay.

It took me almost two months to admit something simple: I was giving my best energy to urgent things, not meaningful ones. Urgent feels loud. Someone needs a reply. A task has a deadline. A message feels immediate. Meaningful is quiet. It waits. Your path is usually quiet. It doesn't demand. It doesn't shout. It just sits there, waiting for you to return.

Someone once wrote to me saying she keeps planning her study schedule perfectly — color-coded, detailed — but rarely follows it for more than a week. Not because she lacks desire. But because something "more important" always appears. When we talked deeper, she realized something painful. Nothing more important appeared. Only more immediate. And immediate wins when you don't protect your direction.

Staying on your path requires small protection. Not big motivation. Not dramatic discipline. Protection. Protection of twenty minutes. Protection of one page. Protection of one quiet effort. Because sideways doesn't feel dangerous. But repeated sideways steps can take you far from where you meant to go.
