## The story

Before becoming a research pharmacist, I had never imagined that writing a proposal could be such a devastating task. Over the past two weeks, I have drafted four different versions, and none of them have worked out. The reason is not that the prospects behind these proposals lack promise — in fact, each one held the potential to make a meaningful contribution to the field. Rather, the real-world scenario simply does not allow me to obtain the data or resources I need to carry the work forward. After countless revisions and discussions with colleagues, I finally settled on a proposal that requires no financial support but demands that I build everything from scratch — including the methodology itself, since no one on earth has ever attempted this kind of study before.

Although I know that if I reach the goal I have set out to achieve, the reward would be far greater than I can imagine, the uncertainty of whether I am truly capable of accomplishing it still weighs on me. There are nights when I lie awake wondering whether I have set the bar too high, or whether I am underestimating the obstacles that lie ahead. This concern grew even heavier when my boss offered a second opinion: he gently suggested that I look for something less novel but more achievable, given the strict one-year deadline imposed by the program. He worried that such rigid time constraints might stifle genuine innovation and ultimately lead me somewhere far from where I truly want to go. His words made sense, yet a part of me was reluctant to give up on the original vision that had inspired me in the first place.

It was not until the end of this week that I seemed to find the middle ground I had been searching for. I happened to come across an article that introduced me to an idea I had never considered before — and to my surprise, this concept could solve the preliminary problem I had been struggling to address in my proposal. The more I read, the more I realized how naturally it fit with the questions I had been asking. If I apply this method and treat the preliminary problem as a "moon project," it would provide valuable insight into the clinical issue and help me form a better strategy to confront the larger problem I am determined to solve. As for my original, more ambitious proposal, perhaps I can regard it as a "Mars project." If I have the chance to land on Mars one day, it will be an incredible journey filled with discovery. But even if I never reach that distant destination, at least I will still have completed a meaningful piece of research by landing safely on the moon — and sometimes, that is exactly where the most important steps begin.

<Callout label="Teacher's note">
This handout is at the Intermediate level. The text frames a real research dilemma using the metaphor of two space missions — a near, achievable "moon project" and a distant, ambitious "Mars project" — to teach students how to balance ambition with practicality when goals collide with real-world constraints.
</Callout>
